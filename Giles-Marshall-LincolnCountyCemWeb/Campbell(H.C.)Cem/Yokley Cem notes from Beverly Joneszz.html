HERE IS TERINA'S REPLY:
I don't mind sharing what I have with Wayne. I can do the names, probably dates, but it would take another visit to read some of the fine inscriptions. It is hard to believe that it's not listed. Most of these folks were prominent figures in Campbellsville history or relatives of such.

This cemetery is on your right if you're heading north to Columbia.
You can see it from Hwy. 245, but you have to turn right onto Smith Hollow Rd. Then it will be on your left up on the hilltop. It sits in a patch of trees, everything else around it is pasture.

I'm not sure who the land owner is. I'm a country girl, so I just went right through the electric cattle fence, climbed up the hill and went right in. Kinda glad I did, seems like the owners may have an issue with people nosing around up there if they are doing things they know people won't appreciate. Needless to say I was ticked off when I saw the state of things. Overgrowth is one thing, unnecessary destruction is another.
By the way, it IS fenced, but a fence doesn't do any good with the gate left wide open.
It does make a person mad enough to sue. : )

Anyway, I'll work on this during the holidays when I will have some time to sit and concentrate. I have a big list of genealogy related things that I want to get finished up and this is one of them.
Hope you have a Merry Christmas!
Terina

Yokley cemetery - at Hwy 245 (Campbellsville Pike) and Smith Hollow Road. It's up on a hill and is visible from Hwy 245. It's in the middle of a farm property/cattle pasture, but is fenced around the cemetery. Lots of graves in it.

The old cemetery book gives directions thusly:
Yokley Cemetery location:  Hwy 64W, right through Campbellsville to Columbia Road. Cemetery on right on hill behind Angus house, in chain link fence. 
 
Terina Boyd, who provided the photo of the stone for Emma Hayes, had this to say about Yokley Cemetery:
I could not get every grave as some were tipped over and too heavy to lift and see who was on the stone. Cattle have been in the cemetery and have tipped over/damaged several stones and it is somewhat grown up with grass and weeds. Whoever owns the farm has also been dragging their dead cows into the cemetery to rot, lots of remains scattered around. There was a fairly "fresh" one in there when I went in, so I didn't stay in there as long as I would like.
There were a few "box tombs" that were very worn or were damaged. Some I could read, and some not.
There were some unmarked graves or graves with just a plain rock and no name.
So I just took as many pictures as I could.
Needless to say, there are definitely a few more graves that still need to be identified in that cemetery. May have to go back in there again.
